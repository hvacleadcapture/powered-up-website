# EV Charger Install in Taunton, MA: What to Expect and What It'll Cost

If you just bought an electric vehicle or you're about to, you're probably realizing that charging off a standard 120V outlet is going to get old fast. A proper Level 2 EV charger install at your home in Taunton gives you a full charge overnight instead of waiting two or three days. Here's what the job actually looks like from start to finish.

## What's the Difference Between Level 1 and Level 2 Charging?

Level 1 is the cord that came with your car. You plug it into any standard outlet and it adds roughly 3 to 5 miles of range per hour. For most people driving 30 to 50 miles a day, that math doesn't work.

Level 2 runs on 240V, the same type of circuit your dryer or electric range uses. It adds anywhere from 20 to 30 miles of range per hour depending on your car. Plug in at 10pm, wake up with a full battery.

The charger unit itself is called an EVSE. Most homeowners in the Taunton area go with a hardwired 48-amp unit, which gives you the fastest Level 2 charging a home setup can deliver.

## What Does an EV Charger Install Actually Cost in Taunton?

The honest answer is it depends on what your electrical panel looks like right now.

If your panel has open breaker slots and is already 200 amps, the install is straightforward. You're typically looking at $400 to $800 for labor, plus the cost of the charger unit itself, which usually runs $300 to $700 depending on the brand and features you want.

If your panel is full, older, or still running on 100-amp service, the math changes. A panel upgrade to 200 amps in Southeastern Massachusetts runs $1,500 to $3,000 depending on the scope of work. A lot of older homes in Taunton, especially in neighborhoods like Whittenton and along Route 44, were built when nobody anticipated this kind of electrical load.

The other variable is the run from your panel to where your car parks. If your garage is attached and your panel is 20 feet away, that's a short run. If you're parking in a detached garage or a driveway on the opposite side of the house, you're adding wire, conduit, and labor.

## Does My Panel Need to Be Upgraded First?

Not always, but it's the first thing worth checking.

A 48-amp EV charger needs a 60-amp dedicated circuit. If your panel is full or your service is already maxed out, adding that circuit isn't safe or code-compliant without first addressing capacity. ## Inside or Outside: Where Does the Charger Go?

Most people mount the charger inside their garage on the wall closest to the panel. That keeps the wire run short and protects the unit from weather.

If you don't have a garage, outdoor-rated chargers exist and work fine in Massachusetts winters. Southeastern MA weather is hard on exterior electrical equipment over time, so the mounting location, conduit type, and weatherproofing matter. This isn't a job to cut corners on when you're dealing with 240V outside.

If you're parking in a driveway off the street, there are also options for pedestal-mounted units. It's worth a quick conversation to figure out what makes the most sense for your specific setup.

## Will This Work With My Car?

Almost certainly yes. Level 2 chargers use a J1772 connector, which is the standard across nearly every EV sold in the US, including Ford, GM, Hyundai, Kia, Rivian, and most others.

Tesla vehicles use a different port on older models, but Tesla includes a J1772 adapter with every car. Newer Tesla models with the NACS port also accept J1772 with the included adapter. Either way, a standard Level 2 install will charge your car.
