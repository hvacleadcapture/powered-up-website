# Standby vs. Portable Generators for Taunton MA Homes

If you want the power to come back on by itself when the grid drops, you want a standby generator. If you just need to keep the fridge and a few lights going and you don't mind rolling a machine out and firing it up, a portable can do that. The right pick comes down to how you want to live during an outage, not just the sticker on the box.

Here in Taunton we get the whole menu. Nor'easters off the coast, ice loading up the lines, and those summer thunderstorms that knock out a neighborhood at a time. So this is a real question for a lot of the homes I work in.

## What is the actual difference between the two?

A standby generator is a permanent unit. It sits outside on a pad, wired into your home, and it runs off natural gas or propane. When the power fails, it starts on its own through a transfer switch and picks up the circuits you set it up to cover. You don't have to be home for it to work.

A portable generator is a machine you store in the garage or shed. When the power goes out, you pull it outside, start it, and run cords or plug it into an inlet. It runs on gasoline, so you're managing fuel and refilling it during a long outage.

The short version: standby is hands-off and always ready. Portable is cheaper up front and more work when you actually need it.

## Which one makes sense for a Taunton home?

It depends on how long your outages tend to run and what you need to keep alive.

If you're on a well, have a sump pump fighting a wet basement, or you've got medical equipment that can't sit without power, a standby unit that starts itself is worth a hard look. A lot of the older homes around Whittenton and the neighborhoods off Bay Street sit low enough that a dead sump pump during a storm turns into a flooded basement fast.

If your outages are usually short and you mainly want to protect food and run a space heater, a portable can cover you. Just know you're the one out in the weather starting it, and you can't run it in the garage or near an open window because of the exhaust.

Think about the trees too. If you're in an area with a lot of mature growth over the lines, you're more likely to see the longer outages where a standby really earns its keep.

## How does a generator connect to my house safely?

This is the part that matters most, and it's where a lot of the danger comes from when it's done wrong.

For a portable, the safe way is a manual transfer switch or a generator inlet installed on the outside of the house. That gives you one legal, safe point to plug into. What you never do is back-feed through a dryer outlet or a homemade cord. That can push power back onto the utility lines and hurt a lineman working to restore your street.

For a standby, I install an automatic transfer switch. That switch isolates your home from the grid the second the generator takes over, so there's no chance of back-feeding. It's the piece that makes the whole thing hands-off and safe at the same time.

Either way, the connection has to tie into your electrical panel correctly. If your panel is older or already full, that can factor in, and I'll tell you straight if a [panel upgrade](/services/panel-upgrades) needs to happen first before any generator work.

## What do you look at when you come out to quote it?

When I walk a property for a generator, here's what I'm checking:

- Your panel: how old it is, how much room is left, and whether it can handle the transfer setup
- Fuel: whether you already have natural gas at the street or a propane tank, since that decides what a standby unit can run on
- Where the unit can sit: clearance from windows, doors, and the property line, and how far it is from the panel
- What you actually want to keep running: the more circuits you cover, the bigger the unit and the more wire involved
- Access and the run: a long wire path through a finished basement is a different job than a short run to a panel in the garage

Every one of those moves the job, which is why I don't throw out a number over the phone. A price without seeing your setup is a guess, and I'd rather give you a real one.
