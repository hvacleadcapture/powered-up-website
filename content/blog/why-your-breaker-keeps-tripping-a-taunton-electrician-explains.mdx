# Why Your Breaker Keeps Tripping: A Taunton Electrician Explains

A breaker that trips over and over is doing its job. It's shutting off power because something on that circuit is pulling more current than the wire can safely handle, or there's a fault somewhere. The trick is figuring out which one, because the fix for an overloaded circuit is nothing like the fix for a short in the wall.

Here's how I think through it when I get a call in Taunton, and how you can narrow it down before you pick up the phone.

## Why does a breaker trip in the first place?

A breaker is a safety valve. When too much current flows through it, or when there's a dead short or a ground fault, it snaps off to keep the wire from overheating.

So when yours trips, the breaker isn't the problem. It's the messenger. Most of the time the issue is on the circuit it feeds, not the breaker itself.

There are really three buckets I sort every tripping breaker into: an overload, a short circuit, or a ground fault. Each one leaves different clues.

## Is it an overload or something worse?

An overload is the most common and the least scary. It happens when you're simply asking one circuit to do more than it was built for.

I see this a lot in older Taunton homes, especially places up around the Whittenton and Weir Village areas where the wiring was run before anybody owned a microwave, an air fryer, and a space heater. Plug all three into the same kitchen circuit on a cold January morning and the breaker will let you know.

The tell with an overload: it trips when you turn on a specific heavy appliance, or when a few things run at once. Unplug some of the load, reset it, and it holds. If that's your pattern, the wiring is fine, you've just outgrown the circuit.

A short circuit or a ground fault is different. That's a hot wire touching a neutral or a ground where it shouldn't, and the breaker trips instantly, often the second you reset it, sometimes with nothing even plugged in. That one you don't want to keep resetting.

## What are the common causes I find on service calls?

When I show up for electrical troubleshooting in Taunton, a handful of things come up again and again:

- A failing appliance. Space heaters, window ACs, old fridges, and microwaves are frequent offenders. The appliance is drawing too much or has an internal fault.
- Too many things on one circuit. Classic in kitchens, garages, and finished basements where somebody added outlets over the years.
- A worn or damaged outlet or switch. Backstabbed connections and loose wires heat up and cause trouble.
- Moisture. Basements and outdoor outlets take a beating from our wet springs and coastal humidity. Water and electricity trip GFCIs and breakers fast.
- Rodents or old damage inside a wall or attic, chewing or crushing insulation on a wire.
- A breaker that's genuinely worn out. They do fail, but it's usually the last thing I confirm, not the first thing I blame.

The location of the trouble matters too. Nagging outdoor and bathroom trips often point at a GFCI issue. Whole-room trips point at the circuit or a device in that room.

## How can I narrow it down myself before calling?

You can do a fair amount of detective work without opening anything up. Start by paying attention to when it trips.

Note what's running when the breaker goes. If it's always the same appliance, unplug that appliance and see if the breaker holds. If it does, the appliance is the suspect, not your wiring.

Try spreading the load out. Move the space heater to a different room on a different circuit and see if the problem follows the heater or stays with the room. If it follows the heater, that's your answer.

Here's the line I want you to hold: reset a breaker once. If it trips again right away, or if it won't stay on at all, stop. A breaker that refuses to reset, or one that's warm to the touch, or a panel that smells hot or looks scorched, means the circuit is faulted. Repeatedly forcing it back on is how wires overheat behind drywall. That's when you leave it off and call me.

## Why do older Taunton homes trip more?

A lot of the housing stock around here was built for a very different set of demands. Homes near downtown and out toward Raynham and Bridgewater often have panels and circuits that were sized for a house with a fraction of today's electrical load.

Add in decades of DIY additions, a hot tub someone wired themselves, a garage circuit feeding a freezer and a compressor, and you get circuits running right at the edge. They hold until the coldest week of winter or the muggiest stretch of summer, then they start tripping.

If your breaker trouble comes with lights that flicker or dim when big appliances kick on, or if you're planning to add an EV charger and you're already tight on capacity, the real answer might be that your service is undersized. In a lot of these cases the fix isn't chasing one circuit, it's a [panel upgrade](/services/panel-upgrades) that gives the whole house room to breathe.

I'll always tell you straight which one you're dealing with. Not every tripping breaker needs a new panel, and I'm not going to sell you one you don't need.
