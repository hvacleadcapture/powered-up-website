# Why Your Breaker Keeps Tripping: A Taunton Electrician Explains

If your breaker is tripping more than once, something is telling you it's overloaded, short-circuited, or failing. Electrical troubleshooting in Taunton, MA is one of the most common calls Micah gets at Powered Up LLC, and nine times out of ten, the problem didn't start yesterday. It's been building.

Here's what's actually going on and what it means for your home.

## What Causes a Breaker to Keep Tripping?

There are three main reasons a breaker trips repeatedly: too much load on a single circuit, a short circuit somewhere in the wiring, or a breaker that's worn out and no longer holds reliably.

Overloaded circuits are the most common. You've got a 15-amp circuit in the kitchen running a microwave, a toaster, and a coffee maker at the same time, and the breaker does exactly what it's supposed to do. The fix there is usually a dedicated circuit, not a new panel.

Short circuits are more serious. That's when a hot wire touches a neutral or ground somewhere it shouldn't, and the current spikes hard. You'll sometimes smell burning or see scorch marks near an outlet. That needs hands on it the same day.

Ground faults are similar but specific to moisture contact. In a place like Taunton where older ranch homes and colonials near the Taunton River have crawl spaces that hold humidity all summer, ground faults in bathrooms and unfinished basements are genuinely common.

## How Do I Know If My Panel Is the Problem?

Older panels trip more often as they age. If your home was built before 1990 and hasn't had electrical work done, you might be working with a 100-amp panel that was undersized for modern use even when it was new.

A few signs the panel itself is the issue:

- Breakers that trip even under light loads
- Breakers that feel loose or won't reset cleanly
- Burn marks or a burning smell near the panel
- A panel that's warm to the touch
- Breakers that have to be reset more than once before they hold

Homes in Taunton neighborhoods like Whittenton or along Route 44 corridors that went through renovations in the 80s and 90s sometimes have mismatched breakers, added subpanels that weren't done cleanly, or circuits that were tapped instead of properly extended. That's not a jab at previous owners, that's just how it went during certain eras of construction.

## What Does Electrical Troubleshooting Actually Cost?

Diagnostic work runs roughly $100 to $200 to show up and trace the issue. That covers the time to actually find the problem, not just reset a breaker and call it done.

From there, the repair cost depends on what's found. A single outlet replacement or a dedicated circuit add might run $150 to $300. A breaker swap is usually $75 to $150 depending on the panel type. If the panel itself needs replacing, you're looking at $1,800 to $3,500 for a full 200-amp upgrade, which is the current standard.

## Why Are So Many Taunton Homeowners Running Into This Right Now?

Here's the honest answer: EV chargers.

The EV charger boom hit Southeastern Massachusetts hard over the last two years, and Taunton is no exception. A Level 2 charger for a Tesla, a Rivian, or any plug-in hybrid pulls 30 to 50 amps on its own. If your panel was already at 100 amps and running a normal house, adding a charger without upgrading the panel is exactly how you end up with a breaker that trips every time you plug in your car overnight.

Micah has walked through this situation in Raynham, Bridgewater, and Easton too, not just Taunton. Homeowners bought an EV, had a charger installed by whoever did it cheapest, and now have a panel that's being pushed past what it can reliably handle.

The right sequence is to assess the panel first, then add the charger. If you're already past that point, the fix is a panel upgrade before the charger does more damage.

## Can I Just Reset the Breaker and Wait?

You can, but you're not solving anything. A breaker that trips is doing its job, which is protecting your wiring from heat buildup that could cause a fire. If you reset it and it trips again, the load hasn't changed. The problem is still there.

What does get worse over time is the breaker itself. Every trip cycles the internal mechanism, and older breakers can start failing to hold even under normal load once they've been stressed enough. At that point, you've got a breaker that should trip but doesn't, which is more dangerous than one that trips too often.

Don't tape a breaker in the on position. That one's worth saying plainly.
